What are the environmental consequences of computer device production?

Carbon Emissions

Resource Extraction

  • Lifecycle Emissions: The carbon footprint of a device essentially consists of extraction of raw materials, production itself, and transportation, alongside eventual disposal.

How does computer use harm the environment?

Energy Consumption

Electronic Waste

  • Electric Use: Computers, especially desktop and gaming computers, use up a lot of electricity. Using electricity from non-renewable energy sources such as coal or natural gas contributes to greenhouse gas emissions.
  • Data Centers: Every online activity, be it streaming, cloud storage, or web browsing, relies on data centers housing servers. These centers consume a lot of energy and generate heat, which requires more energy in the form of cooling systems. Data centers are estimated to account for 1-2% of global electricity usage.
  • Short Lifespan of Devices: Regular updates to newer models result in many outdated devices. Improper disposal of e-waste can release toxic materials like lead, mercury, and cadmium into the environment.
  • Lack Of Recycling: Most of the people do not recycle old devices; hence, non-biodegradable parts pile up in landfills.

What are the negative effects of computer device disposal?

Environmental Pollution

Greenhouse Gas Emissions

  • Toxic Chemicals in Landfills: Chemicals like lead, mercury, cadmium, and flame retardants can leach into soil and groundwater, contaminating the ecosystem.
  • Lithium in batteries can ignite chemical fires in landfills, releasing harmful fumes and greenhouse gases.
  • Energy Waste: It's energy-intensive to manufacture devices. Disposing of devices prematurely means increasing demand for new production and, consequently, indirectly contributing to higher carbon emissions.
  • Methane from Landfills: Decomposition of organic waste components like plastics in e-waste in landfills releases methane, a very potent GHG.
